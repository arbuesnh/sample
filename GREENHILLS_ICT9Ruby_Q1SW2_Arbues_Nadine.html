CHARACTERS IN NOLI ME TANGERE
Juan Crisóstomo Ibarra y Magsalin (Ibarra) Ibarra is a young, well-to-do mestizo who has recently returned to the Philippines from seven years of study in Europe. He is smart, well-respected, and very idealistic. As a result of his very liberal education and connections, the priests of San Diego all view him with profound suspicion. His father, the similarly idealistic Don Rafael, was imprisoned and eventually killed after being called a subversive and a heretic by the corrupt clergy. Ibarra intends to establish a school in San Diego in order to carry out his father's wishes and ideas, but due to a plot orchestrated by the cunning Father Salv, he is caught up in church disputes and is compelled to leave the city. Ibarra normally prefers to work within existing structures to improve the Philippines rather than overturn them, in contrast to his more radical buddy Elas, but as the narrative goes on, he starts to adopt Elas's viewpoints.

María Clara She is a woman of great social status and is believed to be Father Dámaso's goddaughter as well as the daughter of Capitan Tiago. She is truly Father Dámaso's biological child, the result of their scandalous connection with Capitan Tiago's wife. Ibarra and Maria Clara were childhood friends, but Father Dámaso forbade their engagement. Ibarra, who has been excommunicated from the church, attempts to comply with her guardians' plan to marry Linares, a wealthy young man of Spanish origin, in order to protect her weak-willed father, Capitan Tiago. She is forced to turn up the letters Ibarra has written her as proof of his guilt when he is placed on trial for sedition. After learning of Ibarra's alleged death, she ultimately decides against being married to Linares and enters a convent.

Father Damaso Father Dámaso is an elderly, ruthlessly corrupt, and Spanish priest who has spent almost two decades residing among the local Filipinos. He has spent all that time among them, but the years have done nothing to make him likeable or to give him any sympathy for his "flock." He is deeply racist, as well as petty and vindictive, and he thinks nothing of using his considerable influence to ruin the lives of those who have wronged him, no matter how small the offense. He orchestrated Don Rafael Ibarra's demise before sarcastically mocking the younger Ibarra.

Elías Elías is a strange guy who, despite his fervent religious beliefs, is on the run from the law and hates both the Catholic Church and the Spanish colonial administration. He meets the more composed Ibarra after the latter valiantly saves him from a crocodile. Throughout the second half of the book, Elas collaborates closely with Ibarra as he uncovers a plan against his life. Elias takes a more revolutionary attitude throughout their several lengthy discussions about the ethics of politics and government.

Father Salvi A more shrewd, younger Spanish priest takes over Father Damaso's position as San Diego's friar curate. He is in many ways more dangerous than his predecessor because he is a more skillful tactician who exploits his religious function for both personal vengeance and political power. He constantly quarrels for control with the town's ensign. He plays the most important part in the book through his scheme to discredit Ibarra, who is in love with Mara Clara and betrothed to her.

Captain Tiago The nameless head of the Civil Guard of the township of San Diego. A man of Spanish descent, he is in a constant bitter feud with Father Salví to gain power in the town. He imposes curfews that make it all but impossible for the citizens of San Diego to attend mass at the proper schedule. He drinks excessively and is married to Doña Consolación, who he frequently fights with.
